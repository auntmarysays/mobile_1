There are many factors affecting outcomes for both the conventional CPR and the eCPR patient. Studies suggest age is definitely a factor when looking at survival numbers. Those between the ages of 18 and 74 had the best survival. Downtime without blood flow is another important variable when comparing outcomes. While gender has been evaluated, it does not appear to be a factor in survival when using conventional or extracorporeal CPR. In 2008, Chen et. al. reported that eCPR significantly increased survival compared to conventional CPR. Since then, the number of ECMO centers performing eCPR has increased dramatically.

Decreased limb perfusion is a major complication of femoral artery cannulation. The distal perfusion cannula is oriented toward the toes on the arterial cannulation side and supplies that limb with arterial blood. It is not required for all eCPR cases, but is beneficial in femoral artery cannulations.

In 2015, Stub et al. reported the results of the CHEER trial. The CHEER trial was a mechanical CPR, hypothermia, ECMO and early reperfusion study. This was a prospective observational study conducted at the Alfred Hospital in Australia. Patients selected for the study were both in and out-of-house refractory cardiac arrest patients involving conventional CPR. These patients were given 30 mL/kg of iced saline intravenously. The patients were then placed on femoral artery and femoral vein VA ECMO. Subsequently, any patient that was suspected to have a coronary artery occlusion was transferred to the cardiac catheterization lab for coronary angiography. Survival to discharge with full neurologic recovery occurred in 14 of 26 eligible patients, or 54% of the study population.
